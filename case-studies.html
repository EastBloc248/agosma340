Case Study

Ukraine

High-intensity warfare and the climate: operational fuel demand, infrastructure damage, and emerging battlefield waste.

Overview

The war in Ukraine illustrates how modern conflict generates both direct and indirect environmental impacts. Beyond battlefield emissions, attacks on energy systems, industrial sites, and transport infrastructure create pollution hotspots and additional emissions through reconstruction. At the same time, data gaps and military secrecy make precise accounting difficult—meaning the true climate cost is likely under-estimated.

Key mechanisms
  • Operational emissions: fuel-intensive logistics, armour, aircraft, and generators.
  • Infrastructure damage: disruptions to power, industry and transport increase emissions and pollution.
  • Reconstruction wave: steel/cement rebuilding locks in significant embodied emissions.
  • New waste streams: fibre-optic FPV drones leave kilometres of cable, adding long-lived plastic debris.
Why it matters

Ukraine shows that “war emissions” are not only a short-term spike—many impacts persist through damaged ecosystems, contaminated soils and water, and carbon-intensive rebuilding. Treating these impacts as measurable and reportable is essential for accountability and post-conflict recovery planning.

Case Study

Syria

Protracted conflict and slow environmental collapse: air pollution, land degradation, and long-term contamination.

Overview

Syria highlights the long-term ecological consequences of protracted conflict, where damage accumulates over years. Environmental harm is produced not only by active fighting, but by institutional collapse, reduced oversight, and disruptions to basic services—especially water and waste management. These dynamics create contamination that can persist for decades and complicate post-conflict recovery.

Key mechanisms
  • Air pollution: burning sites, damaged infrastructure and fires degrade air quality.
  • Water contamination: damaged systems and debris increase exposure to pollutants.
  • Land degradation: military activity, displacement and resource pressure stress ecosystems.
  • Governance collapse: environmental protection becomes secondary, delaying clean-up and restoration.
Why it matters

Syria shows that environmental harm is often “slow violence”: it persists long after headlines move on. Recovery therefore requires more than rebuilding—ecological restoration and pollution monitoring become central to long-term stability, public health, and climate resilience.
